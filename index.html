Introduction

Some people prefer to use dietary supplements for brain and mental health. Is that safe? How effective is that?

In general, there are some pros and cons that apply to all over-the-counter dietary supplements:

Pros

  • Accessible.
  • May be more affordable.
  • Less stigma
  • May have fewer side effects.
  • Percieved as more "natural".

Cons

  • Unknown interactions.
  • Lack of FDA regulation.
  • May have misleading advertising.
  • Lack of study.

Magnesium

Due to the rise in food processing, most adults in the US do not get enough magnesium from meals. This is a problem because magnesium plays an important role in many processes, including the health of muscles and nerves.

Magnesium deficiency can lead to:

  • Muscle cramps
  • Anxiety
  • Depression
  • Insomnia

Testing for magnesium levels can be difficult because a lot of the body's magnesium is stored in bones and not in the bloodstream. Therefore, a blood test may not be an accurate indicator of magnesium levels.

Using over the counter magnesium supplements can be tricky. There are many forms of magnesium supplements, and each has different ability to be absorped by the human gut. For example:

  • Magnesium Oxide has very poor absorption rate and tends to have a laxative effect
  • Magnesium Citrate has higher chance of absorption and crossing the blood brain barrier.
  • Magnesium Glycinate is relatively new, but it shows promise of good absorption.

Magnesium supplements have few side effects. Generally, diarrhea is the worst that can happen if you ingest too much magnesium.
